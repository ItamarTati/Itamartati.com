The One Way AI Has Completely Changed Job Applications

Back in 2021, the job market rewarded decent candidates who simply showed up. Today, AI has raised the baseline so high that only precision and intentionality win.

Back in 2021, before AI became mainstream, getting a software engineering job was… kind of chaotic — in a good way for some of us.

I landed my first real software engineering role at a cybersecurity company that year. The job description was clear: they wanted a full-stack developer with strong Angular and Java experience. My CV? Almost entirely React and Node.js on the front-end. Zero Angular. Zero Java. I didn’t even try to explain why I’d be a good cultural fit. Honestly, my resume barely connected the dots on why anyone should hire me for that specific role.

And I still got the job.

Why? Because almost everyone else’s resume looked just as disconnected. Recruiters and hiring managers were drowning in generic, poorly tailored applications. A decent candidate who showed up on time for the interview often stood out — not because their CV was perfect, but because most weren’t even in the same ballpark.

Fast forward to today — that world is gone.

Thanks to AI, literally anyone can now generate a perfectly tailored resume in seconds. They can ask ChatGPT (or Grok) to rewrite their experience to match the exact tech stack in the job description. They can have AI suggest (or even generate) plausible-sounding projects using Angular, Java, or whatever else the company uses — sometimes in minutes.

The bar isn’t just higher now. It’s completely different.

You can no longer get away with a generic, “meh” resume that vaguely hopes someone sees your potential. The baseline expectation is now a resume that clearly and specifically answers two questions:

  • Do you (convincingly) have experience with the right technology?
  • Are you actually a good fit for this team and company?

If the job description says “we value team players who communicate clearly,” you’d better have a real example — like the time you stayed late to walk your team through a tricky part of the codebase, or built a wiki so new hires could onboard faster.

You don’t necessarily have to let AI write your entire resume (that’s a different conversation — one I’ll write about soon), but you do have to be deeply intentional now.

That means:

  • Reading every job description carefully
  • Writing (or heavily customizing) a new resume for every serious application
  • Proving — with specific, credible stories — that you have the skills and fit the culture

The era of spray-and-pray applications is dead.

Welcome to the age of precision — whether you like it or not.
